JFR-rg: A New Macroeconomic Framework for High-Debt, Low-Growth Economies under Financial Repression
Hirofumi Wakimoto  ·  arXiv ·  2026
New formalization of how Japan stabilized 240% debt/GDP through financial repression: epsilon_t (inflation minus natural rate) acts as observable bias keeping r < g. Introduces three mechanisms: the Debt Sustainability Corridor (epsilon-growth space where stability holds), Normalization Ratchet (shows temporary policy errors lock in higher debt paths permanently via path dependence), and Captive Financial System parameter (quantifies domestic bond absorption capacity). Directly relevant to Malcolm's Lyn Alden debt-cycle framing: this is the first rigorous model of how advanced economies can run hot fiscal without crisis IF they control their nominal rate structure—and what breaks when they can't. The exchange-rate non-linearity and corridor boundaries give falsifiable thresholds for when repression fails.
If epsilon compression continues (BOJ tightening while inflation cools), Japan exits the corridor from below—watch JGB curve steepening past 150bp 2s10s as the tripwire. Counterfactual: if nominal GDP growth sustains above 3% while epsilon stays positive, the model says debt stabilizes without crisis even at 250% debt/GDP—making Japan the proof-case that fiscal dominance can persist longer than markets price.

FEDS Paper: Monetary Policy under Multiple Financing Constraints
FRB: Working Papers · 
Fed researchers demonstrate that firms facing multiple financing constraints (debt covenants, collateral requirements, cash flow tests) respond asymmetrically to monetary policy: tightening transmits strongly because the *tightest* constraint binds first and cascades, while easing transmits weakly because only the *loosest* constraint relaxes. Empirical evidence from U.S. firm data confirms borrowing/investment contracts sharply when rates rise but expands sluggishly when rates fall. Embedded in a New Keynesian model, this suggests the Fed's tightening since 2022 had outsized real effects, but the eventual easing cycle will disappoint growth expectations—critical for positioning around the 'soft landing' consensus and understanding why credit conditions may stay tight even as policy rate cuts begin.
If this asymmetry holds structurally, watch for corporate capex and leveraged buyout activity to lag policy easing by 6-12 months longer than historical averages suggest. Counterfactual: if credit spreads compress rapidly post-first-cut, the multi-constraint thesis is weaker than traditional models.

Routine Work, Firm Boundaries, and the Rise of Local Supplier Entry
Duha T. Altindag, Nabamita Dutta, John M. Nunley  ·  arXiv ·  2026
U.S. saw 40% more business applications 2005-2019 but half the conversion to employer firms. Core mechanism: routine-cognitive work (structured, measurable) became outsourceable to local micro-suppliers without requiring employment relationships. Higher baseline routine-work regions saw 27.8 more applications per 100k residents. Entry concentrated in micro-establishments with no quality improvement—evidence this is boundary redrawing (firms contracting out structured tasks) rather than innovation or gig displacement. Directly relevant as AI now threatens to make *more* cognitive work routine/deliverable-based.
If AI productizes more cognitive tasks into deliverable-based contracts, watch micro-business formation spike without corresponding employer firm growth or startup quality gains. Counterfactual: if coordination costs fall enough, re-integration into larger firms instead.

Dissecting AI Trading: Behavioral Finance and Market Bubbles
Shumiao Ouyang, Pengfei Sui  ·  arXiv ·  2026
Experimental markets populated entirely by LLM agents reproduce classic bubble dynamics from human behavioral finance literature—disposition effect, recency bias, extrapolative beliefs—but with a critical difference: the biases are prompt-tunable. Researchers demonstrate that targeted interventions in reasoning templates can causally amplify or suppress bubble magnitude, meaning agent-driven markets won't just replicate human irrationality, they'll systematize and potentially accelerate it at architectural speed. The paper bridges AI capability scaling with market microstructure fragility in a way that hasn't been formalized yet.
If LLM trading agents scale into retail or institutional use without friction-inducing architectural constraints, watch for autocorrelation spikes in high-frequency volatility regimes and asymmetric drawdowns during sentiment shifts. Counterfactual: regulatory circuit-breakers targeting 'autonomous agent volume' as a distinct category.

Mapping the causal structure of price formation in Texas's transitioning electricity market
Shiva Madadkhani, Nils Sturma, Mathias Drton  ·  arXiv ·  2026
Causal discovery analysis of ERCOT wholesale markets shows wind generation has displaced natural gas as the dominant price determinant—effect size 3x larger. Wind reduces local prices but redistributes congestion costs regionally in seasonal patterns, exposing structural mismatch between where electrons generate vs. where demand concentrates. Natural gas retains causal relevance but role is modest and benchmark pricing shifts over time. This inverts standard energy investor mental model (gas sets marginal price) and suggests storage/transmission become the binding constraints, not fuel cost.
If wind's price dominance continues scaling, watch basis spreads between West Texas wind zones and Houston load centers widen during low-wind/high-demand periods. Transmission expansion becomes the choke point for renewable penetration economics—utilities with build rights capture spread, not wind developers. Counterfactual: if new gas peakers get permitted faster than transmission, gas reasserts price-setting during volatility windows.

Private Credit Markets Theory, Evidence, and Emerging Frontiers
Jiacheng Zou  ·  arXiv ·  2026
Academic survey documenting private credit's 12x growth since 2010 by explaining its distinctive lending technology: delegated monitoring of opaque PE-backed borrowers through soft-information processing that banks can't replicate post-Dodd-Frank. Key structural finding: while direct lenders extract persistent spread premiums over BSL markets (the 'technology' works), average fund-level risk-adjusted returns are largely captured by GP fees, not LPs—a wedge that matters as institutional allocators chase yield into illiquid structures. Framework connects incomplete contracting theory to why covenant-lite structures dominate and why this shadow banking layer doesn't (yet) pose systemic risk despite size.
If fee compression doesn't occur as the market matures, institutional capital will demand structural changes (co-investment rights, fee tiers) or rotate toward liquid credit—watch GP/LP alignment innovations and whether '25-'27 vintage funds show margin pressure. Counterfactual: if spreads compress toward BSL without fee adjustment, it confirms commoditization and signals LP return disappointment cycle begins 2027-2028.

The Innovation Tax: Generative AI Adoption, Productivity Paradox, and Systemic Risk in the U.S. Banking Sector
Tatsuru Kikuchi  ·  arXiv ·  2026
Academic paper uses Fed regulatory data + SEC filings (809 banks, 2018-2025) to measure GenAI adoption impact. Finds "productivity paradox": AI-adopting banks were already high performers, but GenAI implementation itself caused a 428bp ROE decline (517bp for smallest quartile) through integration costs. Uses ChatGPT Nov 2022 launch as exogenous shock for causal identification via synthetic diff-in-diff. Matters because this is first dataset-backed mechanism showing *why* early AI adopters might underperform short-term despite being structurally stronger—integration tax hits small banks 4x harder, creating selection pressure favoring scale.
If integration tax thesis holds, expect continued regional bank M&A acceleration as sub-scale institutions cannot absorb GenAI capex/retraining costs. Watch Q2-Q3 2025 bank earnings for ROE compression among early AI adopters versus laggards—divergence confirms cost absorption phase. Counterfactual: if smaller banks maintain ROE parity, the productivity gains are faster than model predicts or cost structure is wrong.

FEDS Paper: AI and Coder Employment: Compiling the Evidence
FRB: Working Papers · 
Federal Reserve economists show aggregate coder employment growth decelerated sharply post-ChatGPT launch (Nov 2022), using O*NET-CPS linkage and controlling for industry-level shocks to isolate an occupation-specific effect. This is early empirical evidence that LLM productivity gains are already compressing labor demand in the most exposed knowledge work category—not theoretical, not survey-based, but showing up in CPS data within 18 months. For Malcolm: validates the AI deflationary labor thesis at the occupational level before it's priced into tech labor markets or widely acknowledged by founders still hiring aggressively.
If coder demand compression is real and durable, watch for: (1) wage growth deceleration in SWE roles lagging employment by 6-12mo, (2) venture-backed companies extending runway by downsizing eng teams without productivity loss, (3) coding bootcamp enrollment/completion as leading indicator. Counterfactual: if this is transitory, tech job postings for SWEs should re-accelerate by mid-2025 as AI tooling creates net-new product categories requiring more builders.

Marginal REVOLUTION ·

At 0.5pp (conservative AI estimate), the math implies material fiscal headroom even without spending cuts. This reframes the AI bull case from 'tech grows faster' to 'sovereigns can carry more debt because the denominator (GDP growth) expands.' It's a debt-cycle overlay on the AI infrastructure trade Malcolm already tracks—productivity as the variable that changes sovereign borrowing capacity, not just equity multiples.
If productivity acceleration shows up in BLS revisions over next 18 months, watch 10Y real yields compress vs. breakevens widening—market pricing in higher growth path. Counterfactual: if AI capex continues but productivity stats stay flat through 2025, fiscal crisis rhetoric intensifies and long-duration Treasuries sell off despite Fed cuts.
