Macro Signal

Gold standard deflation didn't break from scarcity—it broke from the gap between fixed monetary base and exploding credit instruments. Banks issued claims faster than metal reserves could clear them, creating a coordination trap where everyone trusted paper until settlement demand spiked. The system fragmented into regional credit hierarchies that failed serially.

All three picks map this: reservation demand for hard assets when nominal claims multiply beyond clearinghouse capacity, prediction markets as failed coordination tech when common knowledge collapses asymmetrically, phase transitions when payment systems can't absorb expansion velocity. Watch Treasury basis trades and repo fails. If collateral chains lengthen while settlement times compress, you're seeing 1890s fragmentation mechanics in modern pipes. Short complexity, own the settlement layer.

The Reservation Inflation of Hard Money: Gold-Standard Deflation and the Real Expansion of Nominal Claims, 1873-1896
Ran Huang  ·  arXiv ·  2026
Proposes "reservation inflation" as a distinct phenomenon from circulation inflation: when hard money constrains supply, nominal claims (debt, reserves) grow in real weight even as transaction prices fall. Uses 1873-1896 gold-standard deflation to show price-level decline coexisted with expanding nominal claim structures—debt and reserve positions gained purchasing power. Directly challenges the Alden debt-cycle assumption that hard money = deflationary adjustment = deleveraging, suggesting instead that hard money can induce *leverage expansion* via reservation channels. Offers a structural lens for Bitcoin's deflationary regime: if adoption grows, watch debt denominated in BTC terms for reservation inflation even if goods prices in BTC fall.
If Bitcoin enters reserve-dominant phase (central banks, corporate treasuries holding), watch corporate BTC-denominated debt issuance and fixed-claim instruments. Rising real weight of these contracts = reservation inflation confirming SCR. Counterfactual: if BTC volatility prevents fixed nominal claims, theory breaks—circulation dominance persists.

Price as Focal Point: Prediction Markets,Conditional Reflexivity, and the Politics of Common Knowledge
Maksym Nechepurenko  ·  arXiv ·  2026
This paper argues prediction markets shifted from pure forecasting devices to coordination mechanisms that organize collective behavior through public probability signals. Using 2024 election transaction data, it shows market influence depends on signal persistence, trader breadth, and cross-platform consensus—not just probability magnitude. Introduces Signal Credibility Index (SCI) combining variance ratios, two-sidedness, and trader concentration to measure when markets become self-fulfilling vs. self-defeating. The mechanism: visible probabilities become Schelling points that coordinate donor behavior, media coverage, and voter turnout in ways that feed back into the outcome being priced.
If prediction markets increasingly function as coordination devices rather than pure forecasts, watch for: (1) regulatory pressure to treat them as market manipulation infrastructure, (2) strategic trading designed to move behavior rather than reflect beliefs, (3) threshold effects where probabilities crossing 50% trigger non-linear institutional responses. Counterfactual: prediction market accuracy diverging from poll aggregators would indicate forecasting vs. coordination split is real.

A phase transition in monetary function explains expansion without inflation
Ran Huang  ·  arXiv ·  2026
Huang formalizes the intuition Malcolm already holds (via Alden) that QE does not reliably inflate consumer prices: newly created base money can occupy distinct "compartments" with different coupling to CPI. Using a phase-transition framework borrowed from statistical mechanics, the paper operationalizes phi = (reserve balances)/(monetary base) as an order parameter, then shows Japan's 1971–2026 record crossing a critical threshold after 2013 into a reserve-dominated regime. Local projections confirm unexpected base expansions flow into reserves (phi rises), not consumption transactions, leaving core CPI unresponsive. The paper translates Alden's descriptive "excess reserves sit inert" into testable, quantitative thresholds and offers a mechanism for why different monetary regimes produce different inflation transmission.
If phi > critical threshold (Japan ~0.8), further base expansion should leave CPI flat while asset prices remain exposed to liquidity. Watch European and U.S. phi trajectories: a similar phase shift would explain why Fed balance-sheet runoff has been benign for goods prices but fragile for duration assets. Counterfactual: if phi falls sharply (forced deleveraging, reserve-drain shock), expect rapid CPI acceleration as reserves flow into transaction balances.

Sovereign Arbitrage
Doomberg · 
Doomberg frames Canada's position as structurally subordinate in the North American resource/security exchange: the U.S. extracts energy security and continental defense value while Canada bears sovereignty erosion and economic dependency risk. The mechanism is asymmetric—Canada cannot credibly threaten exit (no alternative buyers for energy exports, no independent defense apparatus), so Washington can extract concessions without reciprocal cost. This isn't new geopolitics; it's the explicit pricing of what was previously implicit leverage. For Malcolm: validates hard asset thesis (Canadian resources trade at discount to sovereignty risk), confirms realpolitik lens over rules-based international order, and suggests Canadian equities/real assets may misprice political subordination as temporary rather than structural.
If U.S. continues pricing Canadian resources as captive supply, watch Canadian energy/mining equities for persistent valuation discount vs. U.S. peers. Counterfactual: Canadian pivot to Asia-Pacific export infrastructure (LNG terminals, rail to Pacific) would signal attempt to escape monopsony—track federal infrastructure commitments as leading indicator.

Empirical Evaluation of Deadline-Resolved Information Leakage on Documented Polymarket Insider Cases
Maksym Nechepurenko  ·  arXiv ·  2026
This paper develops and tests a quantitative methodology (deadline-ILS) to detect insider trading in prediction markets like Polymarket by modeling how information leaks before event deadlines. Using documented cases from a 2026 U.S.-Iran conflict cluster, it finds military-geopolitics markets exhibit exponential decay patterns (half-life 2.3 days) consistent with non-public information diffusion, while corporate-disclosure markets show preliminary fit and regulatory markets fail the model (bimodal distribution). The framework enables systematic identification of wallets exploiting edge before public resolution, turning prediction markets into legible instruments for measuring real-time information asymmetry in geopolitical and financial events.
If this methodology gains traction, watch for: (1) Polymarket introducing pre-trade screening or wallet reputation scores to mitigate insider detection risk, (2) regulators citing ILS-dl as precedent for enforcement in decentralized finance, (3) sophisticated actors fragmenting trades across wallets to evade exponential-decay signatures. Counterfactual: if prediction-market insiders adapt faster than detection tools, ILS-dl becomes an archaeological tool rather than real-time enforcement lever.

FEDS Paper: Pretend or Amend? On Evergreening in CRE
FRB: Working Papers · 
Fed economist uses supervisory loan-level data to test whether banks extended CRE loans post-SVB crisis to delay recognizing losses ('extend-and-pretend') or to solve legitimate temporary frictions. Finds banks actually tightened terms on modifications (more income requirements, faster principal paydown) and extended loans performed well ex-post, contradicting the zombie-loan thesis. The mechanism: modifications address liquidity mismatches, not solvency concealment. This matters because it suggests the CRE credit loss wave may not materialize as feared—or at least not via the channel most investors are watching.
If this holds, regional bank credit loss provisions should underwhelm consensus through 2025, and CRE loan performance diverges from property valuations. Watch: delinquency rates on *non-extended* CRE loans as the counterfactual—if those spike while extensions hold, thesis confirmed.

Cascading disruptions in natural gas, fertilizers, and crops drive structural food supply vulnerabilities globally
Pavel Kiparisov, Christian Folberth  ·  arXiv ·  2026
This paper quantifies cascading failure modes across the natural gas → fertilizer → food supply chain using 30+ years of bilateral trade data. Key finding: under full trade isolation scenarios, 22% of global caloric supply vanishes, with vulnerability peaking in recent years as specialization deepened. Few countries control the entire nexus domestically (natural gas reserves, fertilizer production capacity, arable land). The model reveals which geopolitical blocs face total collapse vs. those with structural optionality. This moves beyond 'food security is important' into *where the choke points are* and *which dependencies cannot be substituted* under sanctions/conflict scenarios.
If deglobalization accelerates, watch which countries aggressively backward-integrate into fertilizer production (India, Brazil) vs. those locked into import dependency. Natural gas pricing becomes a second-order food security instrument. Potash/phosphate producers (Russia, Morocco, Canada) gain coercive leverage disproportionate to their GDP.

The Financialization of Proof-of-Stake: Asymptotic Centralization under Exogenous Risk Premiums
Mikhail Perepelitsa  ·  arXiv ·  2026
A macroeconomic model proves that Proof-of-Stake networks asymptotically centralize when external TradFi yields exist. Investors with access to risk premiums (bonds, equities) accumulate staking positions exponentially faster than retail consumers optimizing for transaction utility. As institutional capital floods in, internal staking yields compress toward zero, completing centralization. The mechanism: external compounding dominates native protocol yield, forcing structural capture by actors who can arbitrage both systems. This is not governance theater—it's balance sheet physics.
If PoS becomes the dominant consensus layer, watch institutional staking derivative products (Lido, Coinbase wrappers) absorb >70% of network stake. Counterfactual: if a protocol successfully segregates consumer/investor classes or implements diminishing returns on large stakes, centralization slows—but no major chain has done this.

Did US Worker Retraining Reduce Participant Automation Exposure?
Julian Jacobs, Jordan Canedy  ·  arXiv ·  2026
WIOA, the flagship U.S. worker retraining program (2017-2023), failed to shield participants from automation exposure. Most trainees simply returned to their original occupational field rather than shifting into lower-RTI roles; observed wage gains appear driven by mean reversion ('catch-up' effects after displacement) rather than skill transformation. Only employer-led apprenticeships consistently delivered structural repositioning. The policy implication: public active labor market programs treat symptoms (wage recovery) but don't alter occupational trajectories enough to matter against automation pressure. For Malcolm: if AI displacement accelerates and public retraining infrastructure is structurally inadequate, private sector apprenticeship models and direct employer credentialing become disproportionately valuable—both as investments and as career-path hedges.
If WIOA-style programs remain the policy default, expect widening dispersion between workers with direct employer sponsorship and those cycling through public systems. Watch credential inflation in tech/trades apprenticeships as private alternatives scale; if AI labor substitution accelerates 2026+, bet against efficacy of federally funded reskilling as a stabilizer.

IFDP Paper: Tariffs and Goods-Market Search Frictions
FRB: Working Papers · 
Fed economists model tariffs with search frictions between heterogeneous exporters and importers, proving that standard optimal tariff theory breaks when matching costs exist. Two new channels emerge: low contact rates between buyers/sellers mechanically lower optimal tariffs, and when private search costs exceed social costs (common in complex goods), tariffs destroy more surplus than traditional models predict. Critically, they find search frictions can flip the sign—import *subsidies* become optimal when market thickness effects dominate. Calibrated to 2016 US-China data, meaning parameters reflect pre-trade-war industrial structure.
Trump's sweeping tariff threats (25% Mexico/Canada, 60% China proposals) make this the only formal framework Malcolm will see explaining when tariffs actually lower welfare through search friction channels—before narrative consensus forms.
If search frictions dominate in semiconductors/AI hardware (high specificity, few buyers), blanket tariffs destroy matching surplus non-linearly. Watch for: (1) lengthening procurement cycles in complex goods as evidence, (2) subsidy policy in sectors where thickness matters (batteries, rare earths). Counterfactual: if tariffs work as Trump claims, delivery times should compress, not expand.
