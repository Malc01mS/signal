Cascading disruptions in natural gas, fertilizers, and crops drive structural food supply vulnerabilities globally
Pavel Kiparisov, Christian Folberth  ·  arXiv ·  2026
First systemic model integrating natural gas, NPK fertilizers, and 11 staple crops across 208 countries shows 22% global caloric loss under full trade isolation—and vulnerability *increased* through 2023. Most countries cannot internally close even one segment (gas OR fertilizer OR crops), revealing structural brittleness masked by globalization. Few possess full-stack food sovereignty. This maps directly onto hard asset thesis (potash, phosphate deposits), reshoring fragility, and which blocs hold non-financialized leverage in a deglobalizing cycle.
If deglobalization accelerates, watch: (1) sovereign fertilizer capacity builds in food-import-dependent regions (India, MENA, sub-Saharan Africa), (2) natural gas export policy becoming explicit food security tool (Russia, Qatar, US), (3) potash/phosphate miners (Nutrien, Mosaic, OCP) pricing in scarcity premium. Counterfactual: if fertilizer trade remains stable despite energy shocks, the cascade model overstates fragility—watch fertilizer stockpile data and substitution rates.

Is the Global Financial System Making the Hormuz Shock Worse?
Project Syndicate · 
Argues the global financial system—designed to stabilize emerging markets through liquidity provision during shocks—is instead exacerbating the Hormuz crisis by triggering procyclical capital flight, tightening USD liquidity conditions, and forcing defensive reserve drawdowns. The structural flaw: when risk-off hits, the architecture itself becomes the transmission mechanism for contagion rather than the firebreak. This matters because Malcolm tracks how macro plumbing breaks under geopolitical stress; if the safety net is the accelerant, positioning around EM debt, dollar shortage dynamics, and commodity-linked sovereigns shifts materially. The piece likely draws on IMF/World Bank critique from within, which gives it institutional weight.
If this thesis holds, watch EM sovereign CDS spreads widening faster than commodity repricing alone would justify, and IMF SDR allocation debates resurfacing as political cover for structural redesign. Counterfactual: if swap lines activate broadly and spreads compress, the critique overstates fragility.

The Demand Externality of Automation
Erhan Bayraktar  ·  arXiv ·  2026
Bayraktar models automation as a two-sided equilibrium problem: firms maximize profit through automation that raises productivity but shifts income from wages to capital returns, while heterogeneous households face incomplete insurance and unequal asset ownership. The central mechanism—privately optimal automation can be socially excessive when low-wealth, high-MPC households lose wage income faster than capital ownership diffuses. High-skill complementarity and broad equity distribution flip the outcome; concentrated ownership and rapid labor displacement create demand destruction even as output rises. This formalizes the distributional bottleneck Malcolm encounters qualitatively through macro podcasts—automation's first-order effect on purchasing power distribution precedes its second-order productivity gains.
If AI-driven automation concentrates in sectors employing low-asset, high-MPC labor while equity ownership remains narrow, watch for: consumer credit stress indicators diverging from headline productivity growth, equity risk premium compression despite rising automation capex, and policy pivots toward broad-based capital distribution mechanisms (sovereign wealth funds, corporate governance interventions). Counterfactual: rapid diffusion of AI tooling to complement rather than displace mid-skill labor would show up as stable wage share with rising multifactor productivity.

Did US Worker Retraining Reduce Participant Automation Exposure?
Julian Jacobs, Jordan Canedy  ·  arXiv ·  2026
Jacobs/Canedy analyze 23M WIOA retraining records (2017-2023) and find U.S. public worker retraining programs rarely reduce automation exposure—most participants return to their original occupational field, with wage gains likely driven by mean reversion rather than occupational shift. Their "Retrainability Index" shows employer-led apprenticeships significantly outperform public programs, suggesting current active labor market policy succeeds at wage support but not structural adaptation to automation. This empirically undermines the consensus that retraining is a viable policy response to AI displacement, pointing instead toward employer-embedded skill formation as the only mechanism that moves workers away from routine-task-intensive work.
If public retraining structurally fails to shift workers out of automation-exposed roles, policy will bifurcate: either massive expansion of employer-embedded apprenticeships (Germany model) or acceptance that displaced workers require direct income support rather than occupational transition. Watch for state-level apprenticeship subsidy experiments and whether upcoming AI industrial policy bills include employer training tax credits vs. public program funding—counterfactual is renewed federal investment in WIOA-style public retraining.

Do Venture Capitalists Beat Random Allocation?
Max Sina Knicker, Jean-Philippe Bouchaud, Michael Benzaquen  ·  arXiv ·  2026
Academic paper from École Polytechnique uses constrained random portfolios (matched on timing, geography, sector, size) to test whether VCs outperform chance. Finding: empirical VC portfolios are statistically indistinguishable from random allocation in the right tail—the home-run outcomes that drive returns. Weak evidence of downside protection only. This strikes directly at the LP capital allocation model and suggests VC returns may reflect exposure to a power-law distribution rather than skill in company selection. If true, implications for how Malcolm thinks about early-stage allocation: you're paying 2-and-20 for beta to a lottery, not alpha.
Published May 2026—this is new academic work challenging VC alpha claims at a moment when AI bubble discourse and private market valuations are under heavy scrutiny.
If this holds, GP selection criteria should tilt toward access/deal flow monopolies (YC, Sequoia network) rather than track record quality. Watch for LP capital flight from mid-tier funds into concentration strategies or direct indexing vehicles. Counterfactual: replication studies fail or VCs produce evidence of systematic edge in specific verticals (e.g., biotech, where technical diligence matters).
