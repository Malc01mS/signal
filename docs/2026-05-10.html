Cascading disruptions in natural gas, fertilizers, and crops drive structural food supply vulnerabilities globally
Pavel Kiparisov, Christian Folberth  ·  arXiv ·  2026
Researchers built a 32-year model linking natural gas → fertilizers → crops across 208 countries to measure systemic collapse risk. Under full trade isolation, 22% of global calories vanish—peak vulnerability is now, not 1992. Most countries cannot close the loop domestically: you either have gas/phosphate deposits or you don't, and crop dependency concentrates in precisely the regions that lack upstream inputs. The model reveals which choke points matter structurally (potash from Belarus/Russia, phosphate from Morocco, ammonia synthesis capacity) and how disruption in one layer (gas cutoffs) propagates irreversibly through the next two. This is the plumbing diagram for why food becomes a hard-power instrument when energy/fertilizer trade fractures.
If nat gas trade continues balkanizing (LNG terminal bottlenecks, pipeline politics), watch nitrogen fertilizer prices and wheat/corn export restrictions as the second-order move—countries will hoard domestic crops before they hoard fertilizer. Counterfactual: ammonia production reshoring announcements (especially in India, EU) would signal supply chain defensibility is now policy priority over efficiency.

What Jobs Can AI Learn? Measuring Exposure by Reinforcement Learning
Philip Moreira Tomei, Bouke Klein Teeselink  ·  arXiv ·  2026
This paper reframes AI labor displacement risk through the lens of reinforcement learning feasibility rather than capability overlap. It finds that existing indices (O*NET-based exposure scores widely cited in policy/macro discourse) systematically misclassify which jobs AI can *learn* versus which jobs happen to overlap with current capabilities—power plant operators, railroad conductors, and cargo supervisors score high on RL learnability despite scoring low on traditional measures. The gap matters because post-training RL (the o1 paradigm) is task-completion-oriented and can be steered toward outcomes in ways that pretrain models cannot. This suggests capital deployment into automation may target different occupation clusters than consensus forecasts assume, with implications for labor market structure and returns to physical-operational roles previously thought insulated.
Post-training RL (o1-style reasoning models) is now dominant at frontier labs—this is the first occupation-level framework that accounts for that architectural shift rather than pretrain-era capability measures.
If this thesis holds, unionized operational roles with high procedural structure (utilities, transport logistics, aviation ground ops) face faster displacement pressure than white-collar analytical work—watch capex into vertical-specific RL agents in these sectors. Counterfactual: if RL scaling hits diminishing returns on task generalization (Epoch AI's compute threshold arguments), traditional exposure indices reassert predictive power.
